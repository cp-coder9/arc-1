## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The goal is not to replace municipalities or get political buy-in. The goal is to make Architex so comprehensive, reliable, and trusted in pre-submission quality assurance that municipalities *voluntarily* accept Architex-certified submissions as pre-vetted — reducing their own workload by 60-80%.

**The thesis:** If Architex can do 90% of what a municipal plan examiner does — and do it faster, more consistently, and with a complete audit trail — municipalities will gravitate toward it because it solves *their* resourcing crisis too.

## 2. THE PROBLEM (QUANTIFIED)

### Current Municipal Approval Reality in SA

| Metric | Current State |
|--------|---------------|
| Statutory timeframe (NBR Act s7) | 30 days (60 for complex) |
| Actual average approval time | 3-12 months |
| Johannesburg CPMS backlog (2022) | 5,000+ applications stuck |
| Tshwane (July 2026) | Publicly warning about delays |
| Cape Town BDM | 10-step process, multi-department circulation |
| Primary cause of rejection | Incomplete applications (60-70%) |
| Secondary cause | Non-compliance with NBR/SANS (20-30%) |

### Why Municipalities Are Slow

1. **Under-resourced** — Not enough qualified plan examiners
2. **Multi-department circulation** — Plans circulate to 5-8 departments (fire, structural, water, roads, town planning, environmental, heritage, electrical)
3. **Each department operates independently** — No shared view of status
4. **Paper/manual processes** — Even "digital" systems (CPMS) are document upload portals, not compliance engines
5. **Incomplete submissions** — Most rejections are for missing documents, not design failures
6. **No pre-screening** — Plans enter the queue whether they're 10% ready or 100% ready
7. **Political/budget constraints** — Hiring freezes, unfilled posts, corruption in some metros


### The Insight

**60-70% of municipal rejections are for completeness failures, not technical non-compliance.** The municipality's biggest time sink is sending plans back because Form 1 is missing, or there's no fire consultant appointment letter, or the site plan doesn't show the servitude. Architex already checks most of this.

## 3. INTERNATIONAL PRECEDENTS

### Singapore CORENET X (Gold Standard)

Singapore's CORENET system (launched 2001, upgraded to CORENET X in 2023) is the most advanced regulatory submission platform globally.

**How it works:**
- Unified electronic submission to 16 regulatory agencies simultaneously
- BIM-based automated rule checking against building codes
- Pre-submission validation tools that check compliance before submission
- Coordinated multi-agency response (agencies review in parallel, not serial)
- IFC-SG format standardisation for model data exchange

**Key lesson for Architex:** CORENET X provides *pre-submission checks* that let professionals validate compliance before submitting. The platform tells you "you will pass" before you enter the queue. This is exactly the model Architex should pursue.

[Source: Singapore URA CORENET X Circular dc23-01](https://www.ura.gov.sg/Corporate/Guidelines/Circulars/dc23-01)

### LA County Self-Certification Program (2025)

After the 2025 fires, LA County launched a self-certification pilot:
- Licensed architects/engineers with 3+ years code compliance experience in LA County
- Must carry $500K per claim / $1M aggregate professional liability insurance
- Can self-certify that plans comply with building code
- Eliminates full plan check review — permits issued in days, not months

**Key lesson:** Professional accountability + insurance + track record = trusted self-certification. The municipality delegates checking to proven professionals.

[Source: LA County Self-Certification Program](https://recovery.lacounty.gov/rebuilding/self-certification)

### Australia Private Certifier Model (NSW/QLD)

Since 1998, Australian states allow private building certifiers to:
- Assess plans for code compliance
- Issue construction certificates
- Conduct inspections
- Issue occupation certificates

NSW's "Complying Development" pathway issues approvals in 10-20 days through accredited certifiers.

**Key lesson:** Private sector can handle building control when properly accredited, insured, and regulated. The 2020 reforms (post-Opal Tower) added more oversight but kept the model.

[Source: NSW Planning Portal](https://www.planning.nsw.gov.au/assess-and-regulate/development-assessment/planning-approval-pathways/complying-development)

### India AutoDCR & Deemed Approval

India's AutoDCR system automatically scans CAD drawings against development control rules. Delhi's DDA launched an AI-driven single-window system in 2025 that:
- Automates scrutiny of building plans
- Performs compliance checks algorithmically
- For plots under 500sqm, uses self-certification by licensed professionals
- "Deemed approved" if no objection within fixed timeline

**Key lesson:** Automated rule-checking at scale works. But the Indian experience also shows the risk — Indore had to cancel licences when consultants exploited the system. Robust audit trails and professional accountability are essential.

[Source: DDA AI Building Approval System, The Hindu, 2025](https://www.thehindu.com/news/cities/Delhi/dda-launches-ai-driven-single-window-building-approval-system/article70971528.ece)

## 5. WHAT MUNICIPALITIES ACTUALLY CHECK (The Circulation Process)

When plans are submitted, they circulate through these departments. Each has independent approval authority:

### Department Circulation Matrix

| # | Department | What They Check | Typical Delay | Architex Can Cover |
|---|-----------|-----------------|---------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Town Planning** | Zoning compliance, coverage, height, FAR, parking ratios, building lines | 2-4 weeks | ✅ 80% (land use scheme rules are formulaic) |
| 2 | **Building Control** | NBR/SANS 10400 compliance (structural, fire, energy, access, drainage) | 2-6 weeks | ✅ 85% (core Architex strength) |
| 3 | **Fire Department** | Escape routes, fire ratings, fire installations, access for appliances | 1-4 weeks | ✅ 70% (rule-based, but site-specific for complex) |
| 4 | **Water & Sanitation** | Connection capacity, meter sizing, drainage tie-in, stormwater management | 1-3 weeks | ✅ 60% (calculable from drawings) |
| 5 | **Roads & Transport** | Traffic impact, access points, parking, sight distances | 2-6 weeks | ⚠️ 40% (needs traffic study data) |
| 6 | **Electrical** | Bulk supply availability, transformer capacity, load calculations | 1-2 weeks | ✅ 70% (SANS 10142 based) |
| 7 | **Environmental** | EIA triggers, wetland buffers, tree removal, noise | Variable | ⚠️ 30% (trigger identification only) |
| 8 | **Heritage** | Section 38 triggers, older than 60 years, grading | 2-8 weeks | ⚠️ 40% (trigger identification, heritage register lookup) |

### Key Insight

Departments 1-4 (Town Planning, Building Control, Fire, Water) account for 75% of circulation time and are 70-85% automatable through rule-based checking. These are where Architex should focus first.

### Phase 3: "Municipal-Ready Certificate"

Once Phases 1 and 2 are mature and have proven track records, introduce a formal certification:

**The Architex Municipal-Ready Certificate states:**

> "This application has been algorithmically assessed against [Municipality]'s requirements for [submission type]. All completeness checks pass. All applicable NBR/SANS pre-checks return compliant. Professional team is correctly appointed and registered. All required forms, drawings, and supporting documents are present and cross-referenced."

**Crucially, this is NOT a professional certification.** It's a quality assurance certificate that says "we checked everything that can be checked algorithmically, and the responsible professional has signed off." It's the professional's declaration plus Architex's verification.

#### Certificate Components

| Component | What It Certifies |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Completeness** | All required documents, forms, and drawings are present |
| **Cross-referencing** | All references between documents are consistent |
| **NBR/SANS Pre-compliance** | All automatable code checks pass (advisory) |
| **Professional Appointments** | All required professionals are appointed and SACAP/ECSA registered |
| **Professional Sign-offs** | All required professional declarations are collected |
| **Municipality-Specific** | All municipality-specific requirements are met |
| **Submission Pack Order** | Documents are in the correct order per municipal requirements |

#### What It Does NOT Certify

- Site conditions (requires physical inspection)
- Complex engineering adequacy (requires rational design review)
- Aesthetic/contextual appropriateness (subjective)
- Neighbour consent matters
- Anything requiring human judgment beyond rule application

## 7. TECHNICAL ARCHITECTURE REQUIRED

### 7.1 Land Use Scheme Database

The single biggest data asset needed. Every municipality has a published land use scheme (town planning scheme) with:

- Zone categories (Residential 1-4, Business 1-3, Industrial, Special, etc.)
- Permitted uses per zone
- Consent uses per zone
- Development parameters per zone: coverage, FAR, height, building lines, density, parking

**Implementation:**

```typescript
interface LandUseScheme {
  municipalityId: string;
  schemeName: string; // e.g., "City of Cape Town Zoning Scheme"
  effectiveDate: string;
  zones: ZoneDefinition[];
}

interface ZoneDefinition {
  code: string; // e.g., "SR1", "GB2"
  name: string; // e.g., "Single Residential Zone 1"
  permittedUses: LandUse[];
  consentUses: LandUse[];
  parameters: DevelopmentParameters;
}

interface DevelopmentParameters {
  maxCoverage: number; // percentage
  maxFAR: number;
  maxHeight: number; // meters or storeys
  buildingLines: {
    front: number;
    rear: number;
    sides: number;
    streetSide?: number;
  };
  maxDensity?: number; // units per hectare
  parkingRatio: ParkingRequirement[];
  additionalRestrictions?: string[];
}
```

**Data capture strategy:**
1. Start with the 8 metros (COJ, COCT, eThekwini, Tshwane, Ekurhuleni, NMB, Mangaung, Buffalo City) — they cover 70% of submissions
2. Crowdsource from professionals using the platform (they already know their local schemes)
3. AI-assisted extraction from published scheme documents (PDFs)
4. Version control — schemes get amended, need effective dates

Every municipality requires specific forms. These need to be:
- Pre-populated from project data
- Cross-validated against each other
- Generated as compliant PDFs

| Form | Source | Auto-population |

| NBR Form 2 | SANS 10400-A | Structural engineer's declaration |

### 7.5 Outcome Tracking & Learning Engine

The system improves by tracking what actually happens after submission:

```typescript
interface SubmissionOutcome {
  projectId: string;
  municipality: string;
  submissionDate: string;
  architexReadinessScore: number;
  architexDepartmentScores: Record<string, number>;
  outcome: 'approved_first_time' | 'approved_with_conditions' | 'returned_for_amendments' | 'refused';
  returnReasons?: string[]; // categorised reasons for return
  departmentThatReturned?: string;
  timeToDecision: number; // days
}
```

Over time this creates:
- **Municipality behaviour profiles** — "Cape Town always queries X"
- **Confidence calibration** — If 95% of applications scoring >85% pass first time, the score is well-calibrated
- **Pattern detection** — "This type of project in this zone always gets traffic queries"
- **Professional performance** — Which architects consistently submit clean applications?

Given what already exists in Architex, the highest-leverage immediate work is:

### 3. Departmental Confidence Scores

**Why third:** Even without full rule checking, showing professionals "here's how confident we are that each department will approve" creates immediate value and drives the outcome tracking flywheel.

### 4. Outcome Tracking Infrastructure

**Why fourth:** Every application submitted through Architex should ask "what happened?" — this data is gold for improving the system and eventually proving value to municipalities.

## 15. THE PRINCIPLE: DON'T FIGHT THE SYSTEM — MAKE THE SYSTEM IRRELEVANT

The strategic masterstroke is that Architex doesn't need to change how municipalities work. It doesn't need their permission, their API, or their cooperation. It works entirely in the professional space — helping architects and engineers prepare better applications.

The municipalities will come to Architex because:
1. Their inbox quality improves dramatically
2. Their rejection/resubmission cycles drop
3. Their examiners can focus on complex cases instead of checking if Form 1 is signed
4. They can point to Architex-certified applications as examples of what "submission ready" looks like
5. Eventually, it's cheaper to accept Architex certification than to re-check everything themselves

**This is the bureaucratic equivalent of a technological fait accompli.** By the time anyone asks "should we use this?", the answer is already obvious from the data.
